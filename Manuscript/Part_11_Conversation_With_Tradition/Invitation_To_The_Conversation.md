# Part XI: Conversation With Tradition

Volume I remains the enquiry.

This part opens the door to Volume II.

The purpose is not to decorate the manuscript with famous names.

The purpose is to ask one question.

> **Who, in the history of philosophy, asked the closest version of this exact question?**

The questions choose the participants.

Not the traditions.

Not the reputations.

Not the conclusions.

Only the question.

Possible participants include:

* Madhva
* Jayatirtha
* Vyasatirtha
* Vijayeendra
* Raghavendra
* Śaṅkara
* Madhusūdana Sarasvatī
* Rāmānuja
* Aristotle
* Plato
* Bertrand Russell
* Georg Cantor
* Saul Kripke

This list is intentionally incomplete.

No thinker enters the conversation unless the question genuinely requires them.

---

# Symposium Method

Every conversation follows the same structure.

## 1. The Enquirer

The original question is reproduced exactly as it arose during the enquiry.

Only grammar may be corrected.

The philosophical content is never rewritten.

---

## 2. Current Understanding

What has the enquiry understood so far?

What assumptions are already being made?

---

## 3. The Tradition

How does the philosopher or tradition answer this question?

Primary sources take precedence whenever possible.

---

## 4. Comparative Philosophy

Do other traditions recognise the same difficulty?

Are they answering the same question?

Or a different one?

---

## 5. Notebook

What questions remain?

What new questions arise?

These become the starting point of the next enquiry.

---

# Example

## Can an Eternal Soul Experience Something Contrary to Its Nature?

### Question

If the soul's essential nature never changes,

how can it genuinely experience suffering?

### Traditional Dvaita Response

To be verified from primary sources.

### Question for Advaita

Does the empirical–ultimate distinction resolve this difficulty,

or does it relocate it?

### Comparative Philosophy

The distinction between essential and accidental properties may become relevant.

Would Aristotle,

or later analytic philosophers,

frame the problem differently?

### Notebook

The question remains open until experience,

property,

relation,

and manifestation

are distinguished more carefully.
