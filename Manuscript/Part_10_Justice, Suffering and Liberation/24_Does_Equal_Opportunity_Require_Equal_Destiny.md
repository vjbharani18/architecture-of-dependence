# Chapter Twenty-Four

# Does Every Soul Ultimately Seek Happiness?

The previous chapter ended with a question.

If different souls possess different intrinsic natures,

do they ultimately seek the same end?

The question immediately returned me to where the enquiry had begun.

---

## Question

Does every soul ultimately seek happiness?

---

At the beginning of this enquiry,

the answer appeared obvious.

Everything I observed seemed directed toward happiness.

Knowledge.

Wealth.

Power.

Relationships.

Even suffering was often endured for the sake of some greater happiness.

The assumption appeared universal.

Yet another question slowly emerged.

---

## Question

Suppose a soul delights in causing suffering.

Is it still seeking happiness?

Or is it seeking something fundamentally different?

---

The question became even more difficult when I considered the Dvaita understanding of **tamo-yogya** souls.

If a soul ultimately moves toward darkness,

does it nevertheless believe it is moving toward happiness?

Or does the very meaning of happiness differ according to the intrinsic nature of the soul?

If the latter is true,

then perhaps my first question had been incomplete.

Perhaps every soul seeks what appears fulfilling according to its own **svabhāva**.

Whether that fulfilment is objectively the highest good remained another question.

---

## Question

If happiness itself manifests differently according to **svabhāva**,

does every soul truly seek the same end?

Or do different souls seek fundamentally different fulfilments?

---

The enquiry had quietly returned to its beginning.

Not to repeat the first question.

To understand it more deeply.

Perhaps the question was never simply,

"Does every soul seek happiness?"

Perhaps it was,

"What does happiness mean for different souls?"

---

Another analogy occurred to me.

A grain of rice reaches us covered by its husk.

The husk and the grain appear together.

So naturally together,

that separating them requires effort.

Yet no one mistakes the husk for the grain.

---

## Question

Could beginningless karma relate to the soul in a similar way?

Not because karma is the soul.

Nor because the soul is karma.

But because the two appear together from the very beginning of our enquiry into saṃsāra.

The analogy did not explain beginninglessness.

It merely helped me think about the relation.

Could there ever have been a grain without its husk?

Perhaps.

But that is no longer the grain we ordinarily encounter.

Likewise,

could there ever be a soul encountered in saṃsāra without beginningless karma?

The question remained open.

---

## Question

If karma has always accompanied the embodied soul,

does that mean karma belongs to the soul?

Or has beginninglessness simply made the relation inseparable without making it intrinsic?

---

The enquiry had returned to the same boundary once again.

Beginninglessness.

Relation.

Intrinsic nature.

Manifestation.

None of these questions stood alone any longer.

Each seemed to depend upon the others.

---

## Next Question

If different svabhāvas always manifest different karmic streams,

are svabhāva and karma two different realities?

Or are they inseparable aspects of the same beginningless order?
