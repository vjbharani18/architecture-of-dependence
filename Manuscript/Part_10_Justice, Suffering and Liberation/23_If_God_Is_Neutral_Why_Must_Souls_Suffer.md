# Chapter Twenty-Three

# If God Is Neutral, Why Must Souls Suffer?

The previous chapter left another question unresolved.

If saṃsāra reveals rather than changes the soul,

why must suffering exist at all?

---

## Question

If God is perfectly just,

why should a soul ever experience suffering that appears contrary to its own intrinsic nature?

---

This question did not arise from doubt about God's justice.

It arose from trying to understand it.

If a soul is intrinsically inclined toward God,

why should it undergo experiences that appear opposed to that inclination?

If suffering teaches,

what is it teaching?

If suffering purifies,

what is being purified?

If suffering merely manifests karma,

why is manifestation itself necessary?

---

## Question

Does suffering reveal the soul?

Or is it intended to change the soul?

---

The distinction appeared to matter.

If suffering changes the soul,

then saṃsāra becomes a process of transformation.

If suffering reveals the soul,

then saṃsāra becomes a process of disclosure.

The two possibilities lead to very different understandings of justice.

---

## Question

Could a perfectly just God permit suffering,

not to change what a soul is,

but to allow what it already is to become manifest?

---

I did not yet know.

The question remained open.

---

## Question

If God is neutral,

what exactly does neutrality mean?

Does neutrality mean treating every soul identically?

Or does it mean responding perfectly to the intrinsic nature of each soul?

---

The enquiry had reached another boundary.

Justice itself now required explanation.

---

## Next Question

If every soul receives the same opportunity,

must every soul reach the same destination?
