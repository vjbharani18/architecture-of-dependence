# Chapter Twelve

# Is Darkness Merely the Absence of Light?

The previous chapter ended with another question.

Can something be positively experienced

without becoming part of the soul?

While thinking about suffering,

I found myself returning to a familiar analogy.

---

## Question

Is darkness merely the absence of light?

If so,

is suffering merely the absence of happiness?

---

The analogy appeared attractive.

Darkness is often described

not as a thing,

but as the absence of light.

Perhaps suffering should be understood in the same way.

Yet another question immediately appeared.

---

## Question

When I touch fire,

do I merely experience

"less comfort"?

Or do I experience something genuinely positive called pain?

---

Ordinary experience seemed to resist treating pain as a simple absence.

Pain announces itself.

It has a character of its own.

Yet another distinction now became necessary.

---

## Question

Can something be positively experienced

without existing as an independent reality?

Experience alone did not appear sufficient to settle the question.

Something may be immediately experienced,

yet that alone does not tell me what kind of reality it possesses.

The enquiry therefore remained open.

---

## Question

Suppose a liberated soul once experienced suffering in saṃsāra.

Has the soul experienced something contrary to its own eternal nature?

Or has it merely stood in relation to something contrary to itself?

The difference appeared subtle.

It also appeared important.

---

I noticed that the question was changing once again.

The enquiry no longer concerned only suffering.

It was beginning to ask a more general question.

What exactly is an experience?

---

## Next Question

If happiness and suffering are both experiences,

where does happiness actually exist?
