# Chapter Nine

# What Does Svarūpa Actually Mean?

The previous chapter ended with a question.

If happiness,

suffering,

and even knowledge can appear in different degrees,

what actually belongs to the soul?

Every discussion seemed to arrive at the same word.

**Svarūpa.**

Yet another question immediately followed.

---

## Question

What does *svarūpa* actually mean?

Not as a translation.

As a philosophical idea.

---

The usual answer was straightforward.

Svarūpa means

the intrinsic nature of a thing.

That which makes it what it is.

That which cannot be removed without the thing ceasing to be itself.

The translation was clear.

The question remained.

---

## Question

How do I know whether something belongs to a thing's *svarūpa*?

---

A different way of asking the question slowly emerged.

Instead of asking,

*"What is svarūpa?"*

I began asking,

*"What can never be removed?"*

If something can disappear,

perhaps it is not intrinsic.

If something can increase,

perhaps only its capacity belongs intrinsically.

The enquiry was no longer looking for definitions.

It was looking for a method.

A way of testing every proposed property.

---

## Question

Can happiness be removed?

Can suffering be removed?

Can knowledge increase?

Can experience disappear?

Each answer seemed to narrow the enquiry a little further.

Not by adding new concepts,

but by removing possibilities.

Yet one difficulty remained.

---

## Question

Suppose experience alone belongs intrinsically.

Why are souls still different?

If every soul possesses the same intrinsic characteristic,

where does individuality come from?

The question had returned.

Difference had not disappeared.

It had merely appeared in another form.

---

## Next Question

Is knowledge the same as experience?

Or are they two different things?
