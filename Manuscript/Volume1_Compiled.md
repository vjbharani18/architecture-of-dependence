# Preface

# Preface

This book is not a defence of a school.

It is not a memoir.

It is not a claim of mastery over Dvaita, Advaita, Nyaya, or any other tradition.

It is a philosophical enquiry reconstructed from questions.

The aim is not to persuade.

The aim is to make each question unavoidable before any answer is allowed to stand.

Every explanation, regardless of tradition, is asked the same question:

**What explanatory work does this concept actually perform?**

Volume I preserves the enquiry as honestly as possible. Traditional claims are presented as current understanding unless they have been verified from primary sources. Where verification is required, the manuscript should say so plainly.

The finished work should read less like an author explaining what happened and more like a mind being forced to become precise.

Some questions remain unanswered.

That is intentional.

An unanswered question honestly preserved is philosophically more valuable than an answer accepted prematurely.

# Introduction

---

Every philosophical enquiry begins with something that appears obvious.

Happiness.

The Self.

Knowledge.

Suffering.

God.

Liberation.

At first these seem like separate questions.

They are not.

Each depends upon another.

If happiness belongs to the soul,

what is the soul?

If the soul is eternal,

what belongs to it?

If knowledge belongs to the soul,

what is knowledge?

If liberation removes bondage,

what exactly is bondage?

If everything depends upon God,

what belongs independently to the soul?

The enquiry gradually revealed that these were not isolated questions.

They formed a dependency graph.

Following one question carefully always generated the next.

# Chapter 1: What Is It That Seeks Happiness?

---

# Chapter One

# What Is It That Seeks Happiness?

Every enquiry begins somewhere.

Mine did not begin with God.

It did not begin with Vedānta.

It did not begin with trying to prove one philosophy over another.

It began with a much simpler question.

---

## Question

**What am I really seeking?**

Or am I seeking anything at all?

If happiness is what I seek,

where does it lie?

---

The question stayed with me.

Almost everything I did seemed to point, in one way or another, towards happiness.

Education.

Career.

Relationships.

Security.

Knowledge.

Even the desire to understand philosophy.

Everything appeared to promise that happiness lay just a little further ahead.

Yet I realised that I had never asked a much simpler question.

If happiness is what I seek,

what exactly is happiness?

Where does it lie?

Does it belong to the world?

Does it belong to the mind?

Does it belong to me?

I did not know.

---

Another question quietly accompanied it.

Why was I interested in philosophy at all?

I noticed that I was not merely looking for answers.

I was looking for explanations that remained internally consistent.

Whenever one explanation appeared to solve a question while creating another,

the enquiry continued.

It was not enough for an explanation to sound convincing.

I wanted to know whether it continued to remain convincing when questioned again.

---

## Next Question

If happiness belongs to me,

who exactly is this **"me"?**

Before asking where happiness lies,

shouldn't I first understand who is seeking it?

That became the next question.

# Chapter 2: If I Observe It, Can It Be Me?

---

# Chapter Two

# If I Observe It, Can It Be Me?

The previous chapter ended with a simple question.

If happiness belongs to me,

who exactly is this **"me"?**

The enquiry began with the most immediate possibility.

The body.

---

## Question

If I can observe the body,

am I the body?

---

The body changes.

Childhood.

Youth.

Adulthood.

Old age.

Health.

Illness.

Everything changes.

Yet throughout these changes,

there remains an undeniable feeling that the one who says "I" continues.

If I can observe these changes,

can I really be identical with the body?

The question did not seem settled.

So the enquiry continued.

---

## Question

If I can observe my thoughts,

am I my thoughts?

Thoughts arise.

Thoughts disappear.

Sometimes they arrive without invitation.

Sometimes they refuse to leave.

Yet I can notice them.

I can examine them.

I can even disagree with them.

If thoughts themselves are observed,

can they be the observer?

The same question appeared again.

---

## Question

If I can observe memories,

emotions,

desires,

and intentions,

can any of them be what I fundamentally am?

One by one,

each appeared to become something that could itself be examined.

The body.

The mind.

Thoughts.

Memory.

Emotion.

None of them appeared to completely answer the question.

Something nevertheless remained.

Not as something I observed,

but as that before which everything else appeared.

The witness.

For a moment,

this seemed satisfying.

---

## Next Question

If everything boils down to the witness,

where does multiplicity come about?

Why do I not experience your thoughts?

Why do you not experience mine?

Why does reality appear genuinely plural?

That became the next question.

# Chapter 3: Why Are There Many Witnesses?

---

# Chapter Three

# If Everything Boils Down to the Witness, Where Does Multiplicity Come About?

The previous chapter ended with a question that refused to leave.

If I am not the body,

not my thoughts,

not my emotions,

then what remains?

The witness appeared to be the only answer that made sense.

For a while,

that felt satisfying.

Then another question appeared.

---

## Question

If everything boils down to the witness,

where does multiplicity come about?

Where does the world come about?

Where do thoughts come about?

Why do I not experience your thoughts?

Why do you not experience mine?

---

I did not arrive at these questions by reading philosophy.

I arrived at them simply by looking at the world.

Difference appeared everywhere.

No two people thought exactly alike.

No two experiences were exactly the same.

Even when two people lived through the same event,

they understood it differently.

Difference did not appear accidental.

It appeared fundamental.

The more I thought about it,

the more difficult it became to treat plurality as something that could simply be ignored.

If there is only one witness,

why does reality appear as many?

If there are many,

what explains the many?

The question had quietly changed.

I was no longer asking,

**"Who am I?"**

I was asking,

**"What kind of reality am I living in?"**

That question naturally led me towards Vedānta.

Not because I had chosen a school.

Because I wanted to know how each school explained the same problem.

---

## Next Question

If different philosophical traditions all begin with the same world,

why do they explain it so differently?

# Chapter 4: Why Are Souls Different?

---

# Chapter Four

# Why Are Souls Different?

The previous chapter ended with a simple question.

If different philosophical traditions are trying to explain the same world,

why do they arrive at different explanations?

I began reading them,

not to decide which one was true,

but to understand how each approached the problem.

---

## Question

If souls are genuinely different,

what explains that difference?

Were they always different?

Did they become different?

If they became different,

what caused the difference?

If they were always different,

does that itself require an explanation?

---

As I read further,

one thing immediately stood out.

Advaita began exactly where my enquiry had begun.

The Self.

The witness.

The question,

*"Who am I?"*

That immediately resonated with me.

Coming from a largely Dvaita background,

I found this interesting.

The first serious philosophical enquiry I encountered began with the same question I had begun asking myself.

For that reason,

Advaita became the first system I explored in depth.

Not because I had accepted it.

Because it was answering the question I happened to be asking.

As the enquiry continued,

another realization slowly appeared.

I was no longer comparing conclusions.

I was comparing explanations.

Some explanations appeared to answer the question directly.

Others appeared to move the question somewhere else.

Others seemed to replace one mystery with another.

I still did not know which philosophy was correct.

That was no longer the question.

The question had become,

**"Which explanation continues to remain internally consistent when questioned again?"**

That became the method by which every explanation was examined.

---

## Next Question

Advaita appeared to answer the problem of multiplicity through the idea of Māyā.

What exactly does that explain?

# Chapter 7: What Is the Soul Before It Knows, Suffers or Rejoices?

# Chapter Seven

# What Is the Soul Before It Knows?

Until now,

the enquiry had quietly assumed something.

There is a soul.

The soul seeks.

The soul knows.

The soul suffers.

The soul rejoices.

The soul attains liberation.

But another question now appeared.

---

## Question

Before asking what happens to the soul,

shouldn't I first ask,

**what the soul itself is?**

---

Every explanation I had encountered seemed to speak about what the soul experiences.

Knowledge.

Happiness.

Suffering.

Liberation.

But none of these answered a simpler question.

---

## Question

If knowledge disappeared,

if happiness disappeared,

if suffering disappeared,

what would remain that could still be called the soul?

---

At first,

The soul would still remain.

Experiences come and go.

The soul persists.

---

## Question

If every experience can disappear,

what actually belongs to the soul?

What remains even when everything else is removed?

---

One possibility gradually emerged.

Perhaps happiness does not belong intrinsically.

Perhaps suffering does not belong intrinsically.

Perhaps even knowledge, in its fullest manifestation, does not belong intrinsically.

Then what does?

I found myself reducing the question further.

Not,

"What does the soul experience?"

But,

"What can never be removed from the soul?"

That question became the centre of the enquiry.

---

The previous chapter ended with a simple question.

If everything accidental is removed,

---

## Question

Does happiness belong to the soul?

Or is happiness something the soul experiences?

---

Happiness appeared to vary.

Sometimes it was present.

Sometimes it was absent.

Something that comes and goes did not immediately appear to belong to the soul's permanent nature.

The same question naturally arose again.

---

## Question

Does suffering belong to the soul?

Or is suffering something the soul experiences?

---

The pattern seemed similar.

Suffering also appeared to come and go.

If suffering can disappear,

---

## Question

Perhaps knowledge belongs intrinsically?

Knowledge appears capable of increase.

Learning occurs.

Ignorance disappears.

Liberation is often spoken of as fuller knowledge.

If knowledge can become fuller,

The enquiry continued.

---

## Question

Is experience the only thing that truly belongs to the soul?

Not even knowledge in its fullest manifestation.

Perhaps the soul is fundamentally the experiencer.

The one to whom all experiences belong.

For a while,

Then another question emerged almost immediately.

---

## Question

If every soul is fundamentally just an experiencer,

why are souls different?

Have I answered the question?

Or have I simply moved the difficulty somewhere else?

---

The enquiry had not reached an answer.

It had only reached another boundary.

# Chapter 10: Is Knowledge Different from Experience?

# Chapter Ten

# Is Knowledge Different from Experience?

The previous chapter ended with a simple question.

If experience belongs to the soul,

what about knowledge?

Are they the same thing?

Or are they different?

---

## Question

If I experience something,

have I already known it?

Or are experience and knowledge two different things?

---

At first,

they appeared inseparable.

If I experience pain,

I usually say I know pain.

If I see colour,

I usually say I know colour.

Yet the more I thought about it,

the less obvious the identity appeared.

---

## Question

Knowing arithmetic is not the same as experiencing arithmetic.

Remembering a childhood event is not the same as reliving it.

Does that mean knowledge and experience are related,

but not identical?

---

Another question naturally followed.

---

## Question

Can there be experience without knowledge?

Or does every experience already involve knowledge?

And if knowledge itself can increase,

does knowledge really belong to the immutable nature of the soul?

Or does only the capacity for knowing belong intrinsically?

---

The same pattern seemed to appear once again.

Knowledge.

Happiness.

Suffering.

Each seemed to possess

something intrinsic

The pattern itself interested me.

Not because it answered the question,

I resisted treating every case as identical.

Yet I could not ignore the resemblance.

---

## Question

If knowledge,

happiness,

am I beginning to discover something more general?

Or am I simply forcing different ideas into the same pattern?

---

The enquiry did not yet answer that question.

The previous chapter ended with a question.

If happiness and suffering are both experiences,

If happiness belongs to the object,

# Chapter 14: If Only God Is Independent, What Does Independence Mean?

---

# Chapter Fourteen

# If Only God Is Independent, What Does Independence Mean?

The previous chapter ended with another question.

If happiness,

knowledge,

and even liberation appear to depend upon something beyond the soul,

what actually belongs independently to the soul?

That question slowly led me to another.

---

## Question

If everything depends upon something else,

must there eventually be something that depends upon nothing?

---

If every explanation depends upon another explanation,

the chain appears endless.

Reason itself seems to ask for something that does not borrow its existence from anything else.

One response I encountered, particularly in Dvaita, described this distinction very clearly.

God alone is **Svatantra**.

Absolutely independent.

Everything else is **Asvatantra**.

Dependent.

Not partially.

Completely.

I was less interested in the terminology than in the structure of the idea.

The distinction itself appeared powerful.

---

## Question

Can there be two completely independent realities?

---

Suppose there were.

Would each remain completely independent of the other?

If they possess no relation,

how could they explain the same reality?

If they possess a relation,

does that relation itself introduce dependence?

The question appeared to point toward a single independent reality.

---

## Question

If the soul is eternally dependent,

what changes in liberation?

---

Liberation removes ignorance.

Liberation removes suffering.

But does liberation remove dependence?

The answer I encountered was no.

Dependence does not disappear.

Rather,

dependence becomes perfect.

At first,

that sounded surprising.

Ordinarily,

dependence is associated with weakness.

Yet here,

dependence itself appeared to become fulfilment.

That was a very different way of thinking about freedom.

---

## Question

If happiness depends upon God,

if knowledge depends upon God,

if liberation depends upon God,

what finally belongs to the soul itself?

That question stayed with me.

---

## Next Question

If God alone is truly independent,

what does it mean to say that God is infinite?

# Chapter 15: Can an Infinite Being Completely Know an Infinite Nature?

# Chapter Fifteen

# Can an Infinite Being Completely Know an Infinite Nature?

The previous chapter ended with another question.

If God alone is completely independent,

what does it mean for such a being to be infinite?

As I thought about infinity,

one question kept returning.

---

## Question

If God's qualities are infinite,

and God knows all His qualities,

doesn't that itself place a boundary around infinity?

---

At first,

the question appeared contradictory.

To know something completely often seems to imply that it has been fully grasped.

And if something has been fully grasped,

has it not become finite?

Yet I found this difficult to apply to God.

Perhaps the limitation lies,

not in infinity,

but in the kind of knower I was imagining.

Human knowledge is finite.

Divine knowledge need not be.

The question nevertheless remained.

---

## Question

When we say,

"God infinitely knows Himself,"

are we describing a completed state,

or an eternal act?

---

The distinction appeared important.

If God's nature is infinite,

perhaps His self-knowledge is not something that reaches a final point.

Perhaps it is eternally complete,

without ever becoming exhausted.

I did not yet know whether that resolved the difficulty.

The question remained open.

---

## Question

Can mathematics help me think about infinity?

---

Modern mathematics speaks about different kinds of infinity.

That did not prove anything about God.

Nor did I expect it to.

But it reminded me that infinity often behaves very differently from ordinary intuition.

At the very least,

it cautioned me against assuming that divine infinity must resemble finite experience simply extended without limit.

The analogy ended there.

It clarified a possibility.

It did not establish a doctrine.

---

## Next Question

If God knows every choice I will ever make,

## Question

# Chapter 21: Can Svabhāva Explain Difference?

# Can Svabhāva Explain Difference?

The previous chapter ended with another question.

Perhaps I had been comparing explanatory structures rather than substances.

---

## Question

Modern physics speaks about different fields.

Each field manifests differently.

A photon field never manifests as an electron.

The possibilities themselves appear constrained by the nature of the field.

Could something structurally similar help me think about **svabhāva**?

Not because the two are the same.

But because both appear to ask whether intrinsic nature constrains manifestation.

---

## Question

If souls possess different **svabhāvas**,

could those intrinsic natures naturally determine the range of karmic manifestations?

If so,

perhaps karma is not merely imposed upon a soul.

Perhaps karma unfolds consistently with that soul's own intrinsic nature.

The question remained open.

---

## Question

If the bodies we receive in saṃsāra arise according to karma,

what exactly is mixed?

The body?

The mind?

The karma?

Or the soul itself?

---

## Question

Suppose a soul is intrinsically sāttvika.

Can a mixed stream of karma ever produce actions contrary to that soul's intrinsic nature?

Or will even mixed karma always unfold consistently with its **svabhāva**?

If that is true,

Perhaps karma is one way in which **svabhāva** becomes manifest.

Not because the question had been answered,

Does **svabhāva** determine karma?

Or does karma shape **svabhāva**?

Or are both beginningless,

Another possibility occurred to me.

Suppose every embodied soul enters the same world.

The same material nature.

The same opportunities.

The same field of action.

Perhaps it is revealing the soul.

If that is true,

then embodiment does not create intrinsic difference.

It manifests it.

If that is true,

what exactly is the role of God?

Is God assigning karma?

Is God assigning bodies?

Or is God providing the field within which each soul's beginningless nature and beginningless karma unfold according to perfect justice?

The enquiry had arrived at a place I had not anticipated.

The question was no longer only about karma.

Or do different souls seek fundamentally different fulfilments?

To understand it more deeply.

Perhaps it was,

"What does happiness mean for different souls?"

Another analogy occurred to me.

A grain of rice reaches us covered by its husk.

The husk and the grain appear together.

So naturally together,

Could beginningless karma relate to the soul in a similar way?

Nor because the soul is karma.

It merely helped me think about the relation.

Could there ever have been a grain without its husk?

Beginninglessness.

Relation.

Intrinsic nature.

Manifestation.

None of these questions stood alone any longer.
