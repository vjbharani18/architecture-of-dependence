# Chapter Twenty

# Why Did Physics Enter This Enquiry?

The previous chapter ended unexpectedly.

I had been thinking about karma.

Instead,

I found myself thinking about physics.

---

## Question

Why did modern physics suddenly enter a philosophical enquiry?

---

It was not because I thought physics proved Vedānta.

Nor because Vedānta anticipated modern physics.

The comparison arose for a different reason.

Both enquiries seemed to distinguish between

what exists,

and

what becomes manifest.

That immediately caught my attention.

---

## Question

Modern physics speaks about fields.

Electromagnetic fields.

Photon fields.

Quantum fields.

These are not ordinarily seen directly.

Their effects are.

Could this help me think about karma?

Not as physical energy,

but as a similar explanatory structure?

---

Another question naturally followed.

---

## Question

Physics also speaks about potential.

Energy may exist before it becomes observable.

Matter itself may be understood through transformations such as **E = mc²**.

Could beginningless karma be understood in a structurally similar way?

Not because karma is energy.

Not because physics explains metaphysics.

But because both appear to distinguish between

potential

and

manifestation.

---

The comparison immediately encountered its own limits.

Fields are measurable.

Karma is not.

Physics investigates physical causation.

Karma concerns moral causation.

The analogy therefore could not be extended indefinitely.

Yet it continued to illuminate one question.

---

## Question

Could beginningless karma be understood as a kind of metaphysical potential,

which later becomes manifest through experience?

Or am I forcing a physical model onto a metaphysical idea?

---

Another thought quietly appeared.

Perhaps I had been comparing the wrong things all along.

---

## Question

Am I comparing substances?

Or am I comparing explanatory structures?

---

The distinction appeared decisive.

The enquiry was never trying to prove Vedānta through science.

It was asking whether two very different disciplines sometimes solve similar logical problems using similar patterns of explanation.

The answer remained open.

The comparison itself had already become worthwhile.

---

## Next Question

Can analogies ever explain?

Or do they merely help us think more clearly about questions that remain unresolved?
